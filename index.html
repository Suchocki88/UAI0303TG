Context and Clarity
Chapter 3: Prompt Engineering | Lesson Day | Duration: 50 minutes

üéØ Lesson Overview

üîë The Big Idea

Lesson 3.3 introduces a counter-intuitive concept: sometimes providing MORE context makes AI output WORSE. Through the hummingbird passage comparison, students discover that context can constrain AI to generic patterns, and that strategic sequencing (building accurate foundation first, then transforming) often produces better results than trying to achieve everything in one prompt.

Note on Lesson Type Change

‚ö†Ô∏è Originally Listed as Discussion Day

The Table of Contents originally listed 3.3 as a Discussion Day, but this lesson contains core conceptual content that students need to learn before they can meaningfully discuss its application. We've redesignated this as a Lesson Day.

Students will have extensive opportunity to discuss and apply these concepts during the four upcoming Lab Days (3.4-3.7), where context decisions become practical troubleshooting challenges.

The Pedagogical Innovation: The "Easier to Write" Question

‚úÖ Why This Question Works

The lesson asks students: "Which passage was easier to write?"

Students will assume the shorter, punchier version was easier ("It's so simple!"). The reveal‚Äîthat the academic version was actually easier because it had scaffolding, reference materials, and established patterns‚Äîsets up the key insight about strategic sequencing.

This creates cognitive dissonance that drives learning. Students must revise their assumptions about what makes writing "easy" or "hard."

üéì Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Explain why sometimes providing more context makes AI output worse (the "context problem")
  • Distinguish between situations where context helps versus situations where context constrains AI to generic patterns
  • Apply strategic sequencing: building accurate foundations first, then transforming for specific audiences
  • Recognize their personal expertise as a tool for evaluating AI output quality
  • Use multiple AI instances strategically rather than trying to achieve everything in one prompt
  • Make informed decisions about what context to include or withhold based on their goals

  • Level 2: Understands that context affects output and can identify when prompts have too much or too little context
  • Level 3: Makes strategic decisions about context based on goals and can use multiple prompts in sequence
  • Level 4: Recognizes complex tradeoffs in context decisions and adapts strategies for different types of tasks

üìã Teacher Preparation

Before Class

Materials to Prepare:

  • Display capability for showing the two hummingbird passages side-by-side
  • Whiteboard/chart paper for capturing student observations
  • Student field journals
  • Timer for reading/reflection periods

Key Preparation Steps:

  • Read both passages aloud to yourself - Experience the difference viscerally so you can guide students through it
  • Prepare your own example - Think of a time when providing too much context hurt communication in your life
  • Review the decision framework - Be ready to help students apply the "include or withhold context" principles
  • Anticipate resistance - Some students will resist the idea that "less information can be better"

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Preparation: The Reveal Timing

The lesson's power comes from students experiencing the passages BEFORE understanding why they're different. Do not preview the lesson's main point. Let them read, react, then discover.

If you explain "we're learning about context decisions" before they read, you ruin the surprise and reduce engagement.

Anticipating Student Reactions

Expected Reaction Why It Happens How to Respond
"Passage 2 is obviously better" Students immediately prefer engaging content "Yes! Now think: why is it better? What specifically makes it work?"
"Passage 1 is more informative" Some students equate "more words" with "more learning" "Did you actually learn more facts from Passage 1? Or did you just encounter more words? What do you remember from each?"
"They're for different purposes" Smart observation‚Äîaccurate but incomplete "Great observation! But here's what's interesting: they contain the same information. So why do they feel so different?"
"Wait, the academic one was easier?" Cognitive dissonance from the reveal "I know it seems backwards! Let's talk about why having more scaffolding and patterns can actually make something easier to produce, even if it's harder to read."

üé≠ Teaching The Reveal

Setting Up the Question

SUGGESTED SCRIPT

"You've read both passages. You've noticed they're very different. Now I want you to think about this question:"

[Write on board: WHICH PASSAGE WAS EASIER TO WRITE?]

"Don't overthink it. Just based on what you see, which one do you think was easier for the authors to create? I'm going to give you 30 seconds to decide."

[Wait. Let them think.]

"Okay, show of hands: Who thinks Passage 1‚Äîthe academic one‚Äîwas easier?"

[Usually very few hands]

"And who thinks Passage 2‚Äîthe short, punchy one‚Äîwas easier?"

[Usually most hands]

"Interesting. Let me tell you what actually happened..."

Delivering the Reveal

‚úÖ The Moment of Surprise

Don't rush this. The cognitive dissonance is valuable. Students are grappling with why the longer, more complex passage was actually easier to produce.

Key points to emphasize:

  • "Passage 1 was easier because the authors had scaffolding"
  • List what scaffolding means: reference materials, established terminology, clear requirements, familiar patterns
  • "It's like building with a blueprint and pre-cut materials"
  • "Passage 2 required something harder: transformation"
  • Transformation requires editorial judgment‚Äîknowing what to cut, what to keep, how to create impact

Showing the Two Prompts

üéØ Make the Prompts Visible

Put these on the board or screen so students can see the stark difference:

First AI Instance (Passage 1):
"Write comprehensive academic content about hummingbird adaptation for a college-level biomimicry textbook. Include evolutionary biology, engineering applications, and technical terminology. Ensure accuracy and thoroughness."

Second AI Instance (Passage 2):
"Transform this academic content for high school students. Make it vivid, concrete, and compelling. Cut all jargon. Use short sentences. Create wonder. Make them feel the impossibility of what hummingbirds do."

Point out: These are completely different tasks given to different AI instances. The second prompt wouldn't work without the first providing the accurate foundation.

Addressing Resistance

Student Pushback What They're Really Asking Your Response
"But Passage 2 is shorter‚Äîthat's always easier!" "I'm confused by this reversal" "Shorter doesn't mean easier. It's harder to be concise while maintaining accuracy. Every word in Passage 2 had to be chosen carefully. Passage 1 could ramble because there was room."
"Couldn't you just write Passage 2 directly?" "Why do we need two steps?" "Try it. Ask AI for 'engaging high school content about hummingbirds.' You'll probably get something entertaining but potentially inaccurate or incomplete. The academic foundation ensures you're building on solid ground."
"This seems like extra work" "Is this worth the effort?" "Sometimes it is extra work‚Äîbut it's strategic. When accuracy matters AND you need engaging content, two stages works better than one. For casual stuff? One prompt is fine. For important content? Build foundations."
"I still think Passage 2 would be easier" "I'm not convinced" "Fair enough. Here's a challenge: tonight, try writing a vivid, accurate, engaging paragraph about something you don't know well. Then try writing a dry, academic paragraph about it using a textbook as reference. Which was easier?"

üí° Facilitation Tips

Teaching "Context Helps" vs "Context Hurts"

‚úÖ Use Concrete Examples

Don't just tell students the principle‚Äîshow them with examples they can immediately understand.

Context Helps Example:

"Imagine asking AI: 'Tell me about the game yesterday.' That's way too vague. But if you say: 'Summarize yesterday's Lakers-Celtics game, focusing on fourth-quarter momentum shifts and Tatum's defensive performance'‚Äînow AI knows exactly what you want."

Context Hurts Example:

"But what if you said: 'Write an academically rigorous, standards-aligned, comprehensive analysis of the Lakers-Celtics game using sports journalism best practices and analytical frameworks while maintaining accessibility for general audiences.' By the time you're done listing requirements, AI defaults to dry, formal patterns."

Connecting to Personal Expertise

üéØ Make This Personal

Students need to see how THEIR knowledge matters.

Effective prompt: "Think about something you know way more about than I do. Maybe it's a video game, a sport, a book series, a hobby. You're an expert compared to me."

"Now imagine I asked AI to write about that topic. Would you immediately recognize if the output was generic? If it was wrong? If it was just reciting obvious facts vs. showing real understanding?"

"That recognition‚Äîthat's editorial judgment. And you have it for YOUR areas of expertise. That's why your creative project works‚Äîyou're the expert on your own story."

Teaching the Strategic Patterns

üéØ Don't Overwhelm

The lesson includes four strategic patterns (Narrow‚ÜíBroad, Brainstorm‚ÜíSelect‚ÜíDevelop, Rough‚ÜíRefined, Different Perspectives). You don't need to cover all four deeply.

Pick 2-3 patterns that:

  • Connect to work students are already doing
  • Address problems students have encountered
  • Will be immediately useful in upcoming labs

For most classes, focus on:

  • Narrow‚ÜíBroad (foundation ‚Üí transformation, like the hummingbird example)
  • Brainstorm‚ÜíSelect‚ÜíDevelop (useful for creative project work)

Students will discover and practice the others during labs.

Managing the Conceptual Load

‚ö†Ô∏è This Is a Concept-Heavy Lesson

Students are learning:

  • The context problem (counter-intuitive)
  • Strategic sequencing (new approach)
  • Multiple AI instances (new technique)
  • Decision frameworks (judgment skills)
  • Strategic patterns (multiple approaches)

You don't need students to master all of this today. The goal is introduction and understanding. Mastery comes through practice in the lab days.

If students seem overwhelmed:

  • "This is a lot of new ideas. The important takeaway: context can help OR hurt, and you get to make strategic decisions about it."
  • "You'll practice these ideas extensively in our next four lab days. Today is about understanding the concept. Practice comes next."
  • "Don't worry about memorizing every pattern. Focus on the principle: sometimes two simpler prompts beat one complex prompt."

Assessing Discussion Questions 6 & 7

üìù Question 6: Context Decisions in Personal Expertise

What to look for:

  • Demonstrates understanding: Recognizes that context can constrain AI to generic patterns
  • Makes specific choices: Names concrete context they would include/withhold
  • Shows reasoning: Explains WHY certain context helps vs. hurts
  • Connects to their work: References their creative project or genuine expertise

Strong response example:

"In my story about a Mars colony, I'd tell AI that my character Sofia is an engineer who questions authority. But I wouldn't say 'this explores themes of individualism vs. collectivism in isolated communities' because that might make AI write heavy-handed philosophical dialogue. I want the themes to emerge naturally from Sofia's choices, not from AI trying to sound intellectual."

This question assesses: Application and strategic thinking

üìù Question 7: Scenario Analysis

What to look for:

  • Makes clear choice: Picks one option and commits
  • Explains tradeoffs: Shows awareness other options have merit in different situations
  • Considers context: Recognizes when their choice might NOT be best
  • Uses lesson concepts: References conflicting priorities, strategic sequencing, etc.

Strong response example:

"For the creative story scenario, I'd choose Option C‚Äîtwo prompts in sequence. Option B gives AI too many conflicting constraints (she's uncomfortable but direct, he deflects but is professional, they're both right but protocols complicate...). That's like the 'scary but not too scary' problem we learned about. By splitting it, the first prompt gets the situation right, the second refines for character voices. Option B might work if I already had a really clear vision of the exact dialogue, but Option C gives me more flexibility."

This question assesses: Analytical thinking and nuanced decision-making

‚úÖ Remember: Discussion Questions Have No Single Right Answer

Evaluate based on quality of reasoning, not whether students arrive at a particular conclusion. Look for evidence that they're thinking strategically about context decisions rather than following a formula.

üé® Differentiation Strategies

For Students Who Struggle With Abstract Concepts

  • Use more concrete examples: Sports, cooking, gaming‚Äîwhatever they know well
  • Draw the two-stage process visually: Box 1 (accurate foundation) ‚Üí Box 2 (transform for audience)
  • Connect to their creative project specifically: "In YOUR story, what context would help AI vs. constrain it?"
  • Provide sentence frames: "I would include ___ because ___. I would withhold ___ because ___."

For Advanced Students

  • Challenge with edge cases: "When might withholding context backfire? When might the two-stage approach be inefficient?"
  • Meta-analysis: "How does this principle apply to human-to-human communication? When do YOU provide too much context?"
  • Extended patterns: "Can you design a three-stage or four-stage process for complex content creation?"
  • Research connection: "Look up 'prompt engineering' research. What do experts say about context?"

For Students Who Resist the Concept

‚ö†Ô∏è Some Students Will Push Back

"More information is always better" is deeply ingrained. Some students will resist the idea that less can be more.

Don't argue‚Äîinvite experimentation:

  • "I get your skepticism. Here's what I want you to do in the next lab: try both approaches. Give AI everything upfront in one prompt, then try the two-stage approach. Document which works better for YOUR project."
  • "You might be right that more context works for you. The principle isn't 'always withhold context'‚Äîit's 'make strategic decisions.' Test it yourself."
  • "The hummingbird example worked this way. That doesn't mean every situation does. You're learning to recognize when each approach fits."

For ELL Students

  • Visual support: Show the passages with color-coding (technical terms in one color, vivid language in another)
  • Paired reading: Let them read with a partner who can clarify Passage 1's academic language
  • Translation insight: Connect to their experience‚Äîtranslation often requires transformation, not word-for-word
  • Simplified framework: "Two questions: 1) Does AI need this to be accurate? 2) Will this make AI sound too formal?"

For Students With Writing Anxiety

‚úÖ This Lesson Can Be Empowering

Students who struggle with writing often worry they can't direct AI effectively because "I'm not a good writer."

Explicit messaging:

  • "The hummingbird example shows that editorial transformation is a DIFFERENT skill from academic writing. You might find you're better at creating Passage 2 than Passage 1."
  • "Recognizing engaging content doesn't require you to write it yourself. You already know what's boring vs. interesting‚Äîthat's editorial judgment."
  • "The two-stage approach means you can build accuracy first (with AI's help), then focus purely on making it engaging. You don't have to do everything at once."
